# Case Precedents — Indian Supreme Court & RERA Tribunals

---

## Precedent 1 — Supreme Court of India

**Case Name**: Kolkata West International City Pvt. Ltd. v. Devasis Rudra
**Forum**: Supreme Court of India
**Year**: 2019
**Citation**: (2019) 16 SCC 372
**Bench**: Division Bench

### Facts
- Respondent (buyer) booked a flat in 2006 with agreed possession by December 2008.
- Possession was not given until 2016 — a delay of over 8 years.
- Buyer had paid ₹40,00,000 (full consideration).
- Builder claimed force majeure due to regulatory approvals.

### Legal Issues
1. Whether the buyer is entitled to refund with interest after prolonged delay.
2. Whether force majeure can justify 8 years of delay.
3. Whether consumer forums have jurisdiction alongside RERA.

### Reasoning
The Supreme Court held:
1. A buyer cannot be made to wait indefinitely for possession. Prolonged delay amounts to deficiency in service under Consumer Protection Act and breach under RERA.
2. Force majeure cannot be stretched to cover regulatory delays caused by builder's own failure to obtain approvals before selling.
3. The builder's obligation to deliver possession is absolute once consideration is received.
4. Interest at 9% per annum was awarded from date of each payment.

### Judgment
- Full refund of ₹40,00,000 ordered.
- Interest at 9% per annum from date of each installment payment.
- ₹1,00,000 awarded as compensation for mental agony.
- ₹50,000 as litigation costs.

### Key Principle
> "A buyer who has paid full consideration cannot be kept in a state of perpetual uncertainty. The right to possession or refund is absolute and not contingent on the builder's financial health."

### Applicability
- Directly applicable where builder delays possession beyond 12 months.
- Supports claim for full refund + interest when possession is not forthcoming.

## Precedent 2 — National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (NCDRC)

**Case Name**: DLF Home Developers Ltd. v. Capital Greens Flat Buyers Association
**Forum**: NCDRC (National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission)
**Year**: 2020
**Citation**: Consumer Case No. 545 of 2016
**Bench**: Three-Member Bench

### Facts
- Class complaint by 340 flat buyers against DLF for delayed possession in Capital Greens, Delhi.
- Agreed possession: 2013. Actual possession: 2016–2018 (3–5 year delay).
- Total consideration collected: Over ₹500 crore.
- Builder claimed delays due to NGT orders and construction approvals.

### Legal Issues
1. Whether builder's standard "grace period" clause (12 months) can override RERA compensation.
2. Whether class complaints are maintainable.
3. Quantum of compensation for mental harassment.

### Reasoning
1. The NCDRC held that contractual "grace period" clauses cannot override statutory rights under RERA and Consumer Protection Act.
2. Class complaints are maintainable where facts and cause of action are common.
3. Builder's own project brochure showed completion by 2013 — this constitutes a representation binding on the builder.
4. NGT orders affected only a portion of the delay; remaining delay was builder's responsibility.
5. Failure to maintain escrow account (70% rule) was found as additional violation.

### Judgment
- Interest at 9% per annum on amounts paid, for the entire delay period.
- ₹5,000 per complainant as compensation for mental harassment.
- Builder directed to complete and hand over possession within 6 months.
- Penalty of ₹25,000/day for non-compliance.

### Key Principle
> "Contractual clauses that dilute statutory rights of allottees under RERA are void ab initio. The Act's protections cannot be contracted out of."

### Applicability
- Applicable where builder's Sale Agreement contains "grace period" or "force majeure" clauses attempting to limit compensation.
- Supports argument that RERA rights are non-waivable.

## Precedent 3 — Maharashtra Real Estate Appellate Tribunal (MREAT)

**Case Name**: Rajesh Kumar Sharma v. Prestige Constructions Pvt. Ltd.
**Forum**: Maharashtra Real Estate Appellate Tribunal (MREAT)
**Year**: 2022
**Citation**: MREAT/2022/0341

### Facts
- Buyer entered Sale Agreement dated 12 March 2018. Agreed possession: 31 December 2020.
- Builder failed to deliver until 15 September 2022 — delay of 21 months.
- Buyer paid ₹48,00,000 (full consideration) by November 2019.
- Builder cited COVID-19 for 6 months; remaining 15 months unexplained.
- Buyer sent 23 emails after March 2021 — all unanswered.

### Legal Issues
1. Extent of COVID-19 force majeure exemption.
2. Whether unanswered communications create adverse inference.
3. Compensation calculation methodology.

### Reasoning
1. COVID-19 force majeure applies only for March 2020 – September 2020 (6 months) per MahaRERA circular.
2. Remaining 15 months of delay attributable solely to builder's financial mismanagement.
3. Builder's failure to respond to buyer communications after March 2021 constituted willful negligence.
4. Buyer's payment receipts and bank statements were consistent — no contradictions found.
5. Tribunal applied Section 18 RERA: interest at SBI MCLR + 2% for unjustified delay months.

### Judgment
- Interest at SBI MCLR + 2% per annum for 15 months of unjustified delay.
- Compensation: ₹48,00,000 × 9.5% × (15/12) = ₹5,70,000
- ₹25,000 as litigation costs.
- **Total award: ₹5,95,000**

### Key Principle
> "Force majeure cannot be used as a blanket shield. The builder must prove each month of delay was directly caused by the claimed event. Silence in response to legal notices creates strong adverse inference."

### Applicability
- Directly applicable to Maharashtra RERA cases.
- Sets precedent for COVID-19 exemption limits (6 months max, pre-2022 only).
- Supports adverse inference argument from unanswered communications.
